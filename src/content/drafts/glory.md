The quest of glory is a quest from the old days. To accomplish actions so extraordinary that you are granted eternal fame. To be honored in life, but most importantly, remembered in tales and legends. 

> The Thessalonian you are fighting, he’s the biggest man I’ve ever seen. I wouldn’t want to fight him

# That’s why no one will ever remember your name

Some people say that men think every day about the Roman Empire, but fuck the Roman Empire, give me Greeks heroes over it any day of the week.

Achilles left for Troy, knowing he would die, all for glory. Why? Why is glory so important to him?
He answers this question for us in his speech with his myrmidons: 

>Do you know what's waiting beyond that beach? Immortality! Take it! It's yours!

Immortality. Achilles wants immortality so bad he is ready to die for it.

It's purely selfish. There is no fighting for a greater cause there, Achilles despise the King he is fighting for. There is no sacrifice for his home or his people, he's waging war across the sea for the sole purpose of getting the glory he seeks.

And yet. Despite all that, this kind of drive, of determination, this commands respect.

We can't say the same about another character that seek immortality. 

>After all, He Who Must Not Be Named did great things – terrible, yes, but great.
>	Ollivander, Harry Potter and the philosophers' stone

Voldemort split his soul seven ways to escape death. And as Ollivanders' say it, he did "great things, terrible, yes, but great". Why is it not glorious, then?  Because he wasn't ready to die for it.
